User Analysis

  • Human Factors Analysis

    strongly recommended

    Collecting and analyzing detailed information about the intended users, their tasks, and the technical and environmental constraint

  • Context of Use Analysis

    strongly recommended

    Collecting and analyzing detailed information about the intended users, their tasks, and the technical and environmental constraint

  • Personas

    neutral

    Personas are a usability evaluation method that can be used at various design stages. The most typical time to create personas is at the beginning of designing so that designers have a tangible idea of who the users of their product will be.

    Personas are the archetypes that represent actual groups of users and their needs, which can be a general description of person, context, or usage scenario. This technique turns marketing data on target user population into a few physical concepts of users to create empathy among the design team, with the final aim of tailoring a product more closely to how the personas will use it

  • User Role Map

    slightly recommended

    User roles and their relationships between them are represented by a user role map that might shows the general vision of the system users. These relationships could consist on affinity, hierarchy or a composition.

  • Field Study

    not recommended

    Software that simulates some aspect of human performance within a limited domain. Mathematical techniques such as regression, fuzzy logic or neural networks to model human performance have been found to be able to accurately predict human behavior in a variety of domains, including visual object recognition, decision- making and temporal planning.

    Task network modeling uses flow diagrams to capture the steps associated with some human performance, and distributions of timing and accuracy data are associated with each step in the diagram. 'Cognitive architectures' or 'integrative architectures' attempt to simulate actual human perceptual and/or cognitive processes.

Task Analysis

  • Tasks Scenarios

    strongly recommended

    Instances of use cases that represent day-to-day work tasks. Tasks scenarios are elaborated for the most representative tasks about each user. It is not necessary that each one corressponds exactly with a concrete task observed by contextual interviews because an scenario may add some more interesting aspects about several real tasks combined.

  • Essential Use Cases

    strongly recommended

    At a high abstraction level, a use case is defined in terms of users intentions and system responsabilities, keeping an independent technology and implementation approach. It may be used at the start of the development process without taking important decissions about the user interface and its details.

  • Brainstorming

    neutral

    Brainstorming is an individual or group process for generating alternative ideas or solutions for a specific topic.

    Good brainstorming focuses on the quantity and creativity of ideas: the quality of ideas is much less important than the sheer quantity. After ideas are generated, they are often grouped into categories and prioritized for subsequent research or application. The outcomes of brainstorming are a list of ideas or solutions related to a particular problem, the ideas or solutions organized into groups and some form of prioritization based on attributes like cost and feasibility.

  • Affinity Diagramming

    neutral

    A participatory method where concepts written on cards are sorted into logical groups on the wall

  • Task Sorting

    not recommended

    A method for organizing information that involves sorting a series of cards into groups that make sense to the participants.

Evaluation

Usability Tests

  • Thinking Aloud

    strongly recommended

    Think aloud protocols involve participants thinking aloud as they are performing a set of specified tasks. Users are asked to say whatever they are looking at, thinking, doing, and feeling, as they go about their task. This enables observers to see first-hand the process of task completion (rather than only its final product). Observers at such a test are asked to objectively take notes of everything that users say, without attempting to interpret their actions and words. Test sessions are often audio and video taped so that developers can go back and refer to what participants did, and how they reacted. The purpose of this method is to make explicit what is implicitly present in subjects who are able to perform a specific task.

  • Questionnaires and interviews

    strongly recommended

    Questionnaires used to assess how users feel about the system being tested and to know their general opinion about their experience.
